Two items in this week's PLA Daily coverage stand out from the baseline. One is internal governance. One is operational posture. Both are worth reading carefully.

## What stood out: The CMC's senior cadre directive

On May 28, the Central Military Commission issued "Several Measures on Strengthening the Education, Management, and Supervision of Senior Military Cadres" — a named, structured, 26-article regulatory document covering ideological remolding, Party committee collective leadership, cadre selection, and oversight of principal leaders.

This is not a routine political study notice. It is a formal regulatory document directed specifically at the senior officer corps, published in PLA Daily. The explicit invocation of 思想整风 (ideological rectification) and 政治整训 (political consolidation training) frames the senior cadre corps as requiring active rehabilitation — not merely tighter procedures.

The context matters: the 2023–2024 Rocket Force purge and the broader pattern of senior officer removals made visible that existing oversight mechanisms were insufficient. This directive is the CMC's formal institutional response. Whether it produces the behavioral change it demands is a separate question. What it documents is that the CMC considers the problem structural enough to require a new regulatory framework.

On the same day, CMC Vice Chairman Zhang Shengmin convened a military-wide special conference on ideological consolidation. The two items reinforce each other.

## What stood out: Electronic jamming against a NATO warship

On May 27, the Southern Theater Command deployed naval and air forces to expel Dutch Navy frigate De Ruyter after it transited the Xisha (Paracel) Islands. Spokesperson Zhai Shichen's statement explicitly acknowledged that the response included electronic jamming — alongside voice warnings.

The expulsion itself fits an established pattern. What is worth noting is the explicit, named public acknowledgment of jamming as a routine enforcement tool against a NATO-member vessel conducting a freedom-of-navigation operation. Beijing is building a public record of treating electronic warfare measures as legitimate maritime law enforcement in waters it claims as territorial. That framing has implications for how future incidents are characterized.

## What was routine — and why that matters

The week's volume of ideological-political education content — Iron Army lineage, martyr anniversary ceremonies, patriotic-scientist framing for the Shenzhou-21 crew return — fits a stable and recurring PLA Daily pattern. This content runs continuously, not episodically. It is evidence of how military political work constructs institutional identity. It is not evidence of a changed policy line.

The Fujian Coast Guard patrol near Kinmen on May 26, paired with immediate Taiwan Affairs Office endorsement, extends a pattern visible since the February 2024 capsizing incident: operational action plus political authorization, framed in civilian-protective language. This is the baseline now. Track cumulative frequency, not individual patrols.

## One institutional thread worth naming

Multiple articles this week — the PLA Daily commentator piece on rectification, the 76th Group Army's positive-and-negative list accountability mechanism, the regiment that catalogued unauthorized phone calls from headquarters — all document the same structural tension: mandated campaigns produce compliance behavior rather than changed behavior. The apparatus keeps inventing enforcement mechanisms to close that gap. This is not a crisis, but it is a recurring and this week unusually visible institutional pattern.

## Term to know: 思想整风 (Sīxiǎng Zhěngfēng)

Ideological rectification. A structured campaign to correct ideological deviation and reinforce political loyalty. The term's most famous use was Mao's 1942–1944 Yan'an Rectification Movement. In the current PLA context it refers to mandatory study campaigns, self-criticism sessions, and supervision mechanisms aimed at senior officers. When the CMC invokes this term in a regulatory document directed at the senior cadre corps, it is framing the post-purge problem as ideological in nature — not merely procedural.

## What I'm watching next

Implementation follow-through on the CMC senior cadre directive. Directives appear in PLA Daily; sustained follow-up coverage of inspection results or enforcement actions would be the more meaningful signal.

Further European naval transits near the Paracels, and whether the PLA applies and publicizes the same electronic jamming response. The De Ruyter statement establishes a public baseline. What happens next will help establish whether it is a durable posture or a situationally specific one.

The cadence of Zhang Shengmin's ideological consolidation activities as the PLA's August 2027 centenary approaches. Two related events in one week from a CMC vice chairman is worth tracking.
