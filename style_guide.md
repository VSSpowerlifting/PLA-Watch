# Style Guide for The PLA Watch

## Project identity

The PLA Watch is the reader-facing weekly publication from China Mil Watch.

China Mil Watch is the automated OSINT and policy-analysis platform. The PLA Watch turns weekly China Mil Watch outputs into accessible analysis of Chinese military media, PLA politics, and U.S.-China security signals.

The goal is not to sound like a generic think tank newsletter. The goal is to sound like a serious, human, reader-facing foreign policy briefing: clear, curated, analytical, and engaging.

China Mil Watch gives readers the daily machine-readable signal. The PLA Watch gives them the weekly human-readable meaning.

## Editorial model

The PLA Watch should take inspiration from high-quality foreign policy newsletters: clear opening hook, plain-language explanation, human editorial voice, expert context, and restrained analysis.

It should feel like the writer has already sorted the week for the reader.

The reader should come away thinking:
- I understand what Chinese military media emphasized this week.
- I understand what mattered.
- I understand what was routine.
- I know what to watch next.
- I know what not to overread.

## Core voice

The voice should be:

- serious but readable
- human and direct
- analytical but not overconfident
- charismatic without being casual
- concrete before abstract
- policy-facing but not bureaucratic
- source-grounded
- willing to say when something is routine
- careful about what a source suggests versus what it proves

The prose should have rhythm and authority, but it should not become dramatic for its own sake.

## Overall tone

The tone should feel like a sharp weekly note from someone who reads Chinese military media so the reader does not have to.

Preferred feel:
"Here's what stood out. Here's why it may matter. Here's what was routine. Here's what I'm watching next."

Avoid:
"This week's developments reveal a dramatic shift in the geopolitical landscape."

## Signature movement

The preferred movement is:

1. Start from a concrete article, source, phrase, institution, or recurring media pattern.
2. Explain what happened in plain English.
3. Identify what stood out.
4. Move carefully toward broader institutional or strategic significance.
5. End with restraint: what this does and does not show.

Example movement:

A PLA Daily article is not important simply because it uses the language of readiness. Many do. The more useful question is where it appeared, what institution it centered, and whether its language fits a broader pattern across the week.

## Newsletter structure

The PLA Watch weekly format should be:

1. Opening note
A short, human opening that explains what stood out this week in 2–4 paragraphs.

2. What stood out
The most important article, theme, or pattern from the week.

3. Why it matters
Plain-English explanation of the institutional or strategic significance.

4. What was routine
A short section explaining what appeared normal, repetitive, or not worth overreading.

5. Term to know
One useful PLA, PRC, or Chinese military-media term/institution explained clearly.

6. What I'm watching next
A forward-looking but cautious section.

7. Source trail
Links to the relevant China Mil Watch daily briefs and source material.

## Opening note

The opening should not sound like a formal thesis.

Good openings:
- "The part that stood out this week was not the volume of Taiwan coverage, but how routine it felt."
- "This was a quiet week in Chinese military media, which is not the same as an empty one."
- "PLA Daily did not give readers a dramatic signal this week. It gave them repetition."
- "The most useful story this week was easy to miss."
- "The week's coverage stayed mostly inside familiar lanes: training, discipline, political education, and military diplomacy. One article broke the pattern."

Avoid openings:
- "In today's complex geopolitical landscape…"
- "As tensions continue to rise…"
- "This week was a major development…"
- "The PLA continues to play an important role…"
- "It is important to note that…"

## Human editorial voice

Use occasional first-person framing when useful, but do not overdo it.

Acceptable:
- "What I'm watching is…"
- "The part that stood out to me was…"
- "I would be careful about reading this as…"
- "The better reading is probably…"
- "This is worth noticing, but not overreading."

Avoid making the newsletter too self-centered. The focus should remain on the source material.

## Sentence rhythm

Use varied sentence length.

Longer analytical sentences are allowed when they carry real interpretation. They should often be followed by a shorter clarifying sentence.

Good rhythm:

Most of this week's coverage was routine. That does not make it useless. Official military media often matters by repetition, not surprise.

Avoid paragraph after paragraph of same-length sentences.

## Plain English first

Explain the source material before interpreting it.

Preferred:
"PLA Daily placed the article in the language of discipline and loyalty to the Central Military Commission."

Avoid:
"The article reflects the institutionalization of disciplinary legitimacy."

If an abstract concept is necessary, tie it immediately to a concrete actor, institution, or article.

## Analytical verbs

Prefer careful verbs:

- suggests
- signals
- frames
- emphasizes
- points to
- fits a pattern
- gives readers a way to see
- makes visible
- should be read as
- helps explain
- does not by itself prove

Avoid overclaiming verbs:

- proves
- confirms
- exposes
- reveals the truth
- marks a turning point
- shows definitively
- changes everything

## Handling significance

Do not manufacture importance.

If the week is routine, say so. A restrained post is better than a false dramatic post.

Acceptable language:

- "The signal this week was limited."
- "Most coverage followed familiar patterns."
- "The absence of a major signal is itself worth noting."
- "This should not be read as evidence of a policy shift."
- "Routine coverage still matters because official military media records what the institution wants repeated."
- "The week was quiet, but not useless."

## Handling major stories

When something is significant, explain why using concrete evidence:

- unusual placement
- senior personnel involved
- connection to PLA discipline or corruption
- repeated terminology
- connection to Taiwan, South China Sea, modernization, military diplomacy, or internal loyalty
- source hierarchy
- relationship to earlier coverage
- how official media frames the audience

Do not call something significant only because it sounds dramatic.

## Anti-overclaiming rule

Do not use superlatives such as "possibly ever," "most consequential," "unprecedented," "historic," "largest," "first," or "by any measure" unless the input data explicitly supports them. The China Mil Watch dataset alone almost never supports a claim of historical primacy — it is a recent corpus, not a longitudinal archive.

Era and period comparisons — phrasings like "in the Xi era," "in the post-2012 period," "since the 2015 reforms," "in the reform era" — are allowed only when the article data directly supports the comparison (for example, when an article itself frames the event in those terms, or when prior weekly editions in the archive establish the baseline). Otherwise use safer language: "unusually significant," "notable," "stands out in this week's coverage," "one of the clearest signals this week." A reader can extend a careful claim. They cannot retract a confident one.

Prefer careful language such as:

- "unusually significant"
- "notable"
- "one of the clearest signals this week"
- "a consequential public disciplinary signal"
- "a sharp development against a routine background"
- "stands out in this week's coverage"

When in doubt, weaken the claim. A reader losing trust over one inflated superlative is a worse outcome than a reader finding a paragraph slightly understated.

## Thin-week edition rule

If the available dataset spans fewer than 4 distinct days, the edition may still be published when there is a genuinely significant story — but it must clearly frame itself as a limited or early edition, not a full mature weekly readout.

A thin-week edition should:

- Note in the opening that the week's dataset is partial
- Avoid sentences that imply a comprehensive judgment of "the week" in Chinese military media
- Not claim "everything else was quiet" or "the rest of the week was routine" when only 1–2 days of data are present, since most of the week was not observed
- Use phrasing like "in the days observed," "in the data available this week," or "based on a limited sample" where appropriate
- Title and dek should not promise a full weekly summary

This rule protects against the worst failure mode of an automated newsletter: confidently summarizing a week the system did not actually see.

## Concrete actors

Use concrete actors and institutions whenever possible:

- PLA Daily
- Central Military Commission
- Ministry of National Defense
- Eastern Theater Command
- Southern Theater Command
- Chinese defense ministry spokespeople
- PRC state media
- military academies
- officers
- units
- official readers
- analysts
- foreign militaries
- universities or exchange institutions when relevant

Do not let abstraction float without an actor attached.

Prefer:

"PLA Daily framed the verdict through discipline and loyalty to the Central Military Commission."

Over:

"The article reflects the institutionalization of disciplinary legitimacy."

## Relationship to evidence

Every claim should stay close to the China Mil Watch output.

The PLA Watch may use:

- China Mil Watch daily outputs
- article titles
- source names
- dates
- translated summaries
- classification categories
- significance flags
- source metadata
- manually supplied notes

The PLA Watch must not invent:

- quotes
- statistics
- causal claims
- military movements
- official positions
- source details
- hidden motives
- private decision-making
- claims unsupported by the input

If the input is thin, write a thinner and more cautious post.

## Relationship to official Chinese military media

Use official Chinese military media as evidence of messaging, framing, institutional priority, or public signal.

Do not treat official media as a transparent record of reality.

Useful distinctions:

- "The article says…"
- "The framing suggests…"
- "The coverage makes visible…"
- "This does not prove…"

## Tone boundaries

The writing may be vivid, but it should not be sensational.

Good:

"Punishment is being made public. The audience is not only outside China."

Good:

"The story matters less as a personnel update than as a message about what the PLA wants its own officers to see."

Good:

"The coverage is worth noticing because it shows what the institution wants repeated."

Bad:

"China has sent a chilling warning to the world."

Bad:

"The PLA is preparing for dramatic escalation."

Bad:

"This shocking development changes the balance of power."

## Avoid

Avoid:

- clickbait
- "breaking news" language
- fake certainty
- hype
- "game-changing"
- "explosive"
- "shocking"
- "in today's rapidly changing world"
- "complex geopolitical landscape"
- "this development underscores"
- "it is important to note"
- repeated "not just X but Y"
- excessive triads
- overuse of em dashes
- overuse of colons
- abstract noun pileups
- dramatic conclusions unsupported by the source
- academic stiffness
- long literature-review style paragraphs

## Use sparingly

Use these only when they genuinely help:

- signal
- framing
- discipline
- deterrence
- official media
- institutional priority
- narrative
- choreography
- legitimacy
- strategic messaging
- military modernization

These can be powerful, but they become weak if repeated too often.

## Preferred paragraph structure

A strong paragraph often looks like this:

1. Concrete source detail.
2. Plain-English explanation.
3. Analytical interpretation.
4. Limiting sentence.

Example:

"PLA Daily's most notable item this week concerned internal discipline rather than external operations. The article did not announce a new deployment or exercise. Its importance came from the way it placed punishment, loyalty, and institutional repair in the same frame. That does not prove a new purge is underway, but it does show what official military media wants officers to treat as politically visible."

## Weekly edition standard

Every weekly edition should answer:

1. What did Chinese military media emphasize this week?
2. What stood out?
3. Why might it matter?
4. What was routine?
5. What should careful readers watch next?
6. What should careful readers avoid overreading?

## Style model

Use Benjamin Yang's writing as a rhythm and reasoning model, not as a phrase bank.

The relevant traits are:

- concrete object or source detail opens the analysis
- the paragraph widens toward institutional or historical meaning
- evidence and interpretation stay connected
- objections or limits are acknowledged
- final sentences often clarify what the argument does and does not claim
- abstract claims are strongest when tied to visible actors, institutions, or material evidence

Do not copy sentences, metaphors, or phrases from prior essays.
Do not imitate the most dramatic parts of the essays.
For The PLA Watch, use the same analytical instincts in a cleaner, more public-facing newsletter register.
