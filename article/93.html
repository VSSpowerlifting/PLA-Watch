A Brigade of the 83rd Group Army Organizes Pre-Assignment Integration Training for Newly Graduated Officers

第83集团军某旅组织新毕业军官岗前融合集训
PLA Daily (解放军报) 9 May 2026
View original source ↗
Summary
A brigade of the 83rd Group Army, based in northern Henan, conducted pre-assignment integration training that co-enrolled newly commissioned infantry platoon leaders and helicopter pilots in a shared curriculum centered on air-ground coordination, including joint scenario exercises, cross-specialty instruction, and a culminating simulation exercise involving UAV swarms, armed helicopters, and ground reconnaissance elements. The program reflects a deliberate PLA effort to instill joint warfighting concepts at the earliest career stage, addressing a recognized gap in which branch-specific training pipelines produce officers who default to single-domain thinking—a structural obstacle to the systems warfare (体系作战) doctrine the PLA has formally prioritized.
Translation

A Brigade of the 83rd Group Army Organizes Pre-Assignment Integration Training for Newly Graduated Officers——

Platoon Leaders and Pilots Attend the Same "First Lesson"

■ PLA Daily Special Correspondent Tong Kang, Special Correspondent Xu Junqiang

The brigade organizes air-ground coordination training. Photo by Xu Junqiang

Recently, smoke filled a training ground in northern Henan as a brigade of the 83rd Group Army launched a demonstration training exercise on company-level tactical positional defense.

"When selecting a firing position, one must consider whether the terrain can bring the weapon's maximum effectiveness to bear and whether it can form a cross-fire network with aerial fires…" Inside the command post, new platoon leader Wang Zihao raised a suggestion on positional defense from the perspective of air-ground coordination, which was adopted by the company's chief of staff.

"My ability to quickly adapt to air-ground coordinated combat is thanks to the pre-assignment integration training the brigade organized beforehand," Wang Zihao told the reporter.

Prior to this, the brigade organized newly graduated platoon leaders and new pilots to conduct pre-assignment integration training, attending the same "first lesson" on air-ground coordination, so that through joint air-ground training and cross-specialty mutual learning they could strengthen their coordination awareness and, from the very start of their careers (官之初), firmly establish the concept of integration (融合理念) and systems thinking (体系思维).

Wang Zihao told the reporter that his path to pre-assignment integration was not smooth sailing. At the outset of the training, seeing new pilots in flight suits and new platoon leaders in camouflage sitting on opposite sides of the classroom—clearly separated—gave him an intangible sense of pressure.

During one scenario exercise, Wang Zihao confidently drafted helicopter landing points and assault routes, only to have new pilot Guo Xing cut straight to the problem: "Although this point is in a blind spot of the 'enemy's' air defense fires, a helicopter conducting a low-altitude penetration is easily affected by terrain masking and air currents. Not only would it be difficult to ensure mission completion, it could drag down the entire aerial formation."

Although he felt some embarrassment, the experience made Wang Zihao realize that the significance of integration training lies not in how much theoretical knowledge one grasps or how many operational skills one masters, but in guiding everyone to learn to understand the battlefield and think about warfare from a holistic and systems perspective.

After that scenario exercise ended, Wang Zihao took every free moment to actively seek guidance from Guo Xing, while Guo Xing actively sought to understand the ground element's mobility capabilities, fire configuration, battlefield protection, and other key operational points. Like Wang Zihao and Guo Xing, the new platoon leaders and new pilots participating in the training gradually broke down psychological barriers through candid and in-depth discussion and exchange and through side-by-side practical training, advancing together through mutual learning and mutual reference.

"Integration cannot remain on the surface—it must be fused into the bone (融到骨子里), engraved in the mind. In peacetime, thinking from the other's perspective and training to fill each other's gaps in coordination; only then in wartime can we clench fingers into a fist (攥指成拳) and achieve victory through combined strength." The brigade's leadership explained that during this integration training, all basic physical training and daily education management for newly graduated officers were organized uniformly by the training detachment. They abandoned the previous training model that emphasized single-specialty skills, having pilots assist infantry platoon leaders in formulating ground training plans and having infantry platoon leaders participate in research on tactics for aviation specialties. At the same time, they incorporated operational elements such as fire strikes, information offense and defense, and comprehensive support in phases, embedding real combat links throughout the entire practical training process, achieving the principle of "however the battle is fought, that is how the troops train (仗怎么打，兵就怎么练)."

As the training neared its end, an intense air-ground coordination simulation exercise unfolded. After Wang Zihao commanded the ground element to detect and acquire target information, he pushed the coordinates of the "enemy" air defense fire positions point-to-point to the aerial formation. In the simulated cockpit, Guo Xing received the shared information and adopted the tactic of ultra-low-altitude penetration with full electromagnetic silence (全程电磁静默), successfully breaking through the "enemy" interception network. Throughout the training, ground reconnaissance, unmanned aerial vehicle swarms, armed helicopters, transport helicopters, and other multi-element joint formations were employed; the new platoon leaders and new pilots coordinated with tacit understanding and high efficiency, successfully completing the seizure and control mission.

"Personnel cultivation is 'the most arduous war preparation.' Our goal is to use concentrated tempering to enable new platoon leaders and new pilots to break out of the limitations of their specialties, expand the boundaries of their thinking, forge joint command and coordinated combat capabilities, and build a strong talent force capable of fulfilling the mission of winning on the future battlefield." The brigade's leadership stated that in the next step, they will formulate individualized training plans around the needs of different positions, work hard to close the capability gaps of newly graduated officers, and from the source "implant" in them the concept of integration and systems thinking.

Adjusting Stride from the Starting Line

■ Yang Yue

The Art of War states: "Therefore, those skilled in employing troops move their forces as if directing a single person by the hand." Systems warfare (体系作战) requires tacit coordination among all elements, and mutual understanding is the foundational condition. How exactly does one achieve that understanding? The approach of a brigade of the 83rd Group Army offers us enlightenment—start from the very beginning.

As a key combination for three-dimensional assault on the battlefield, aerial combat aircraft and ground infantry fighting hand in hand is a required course for pilots and ground element commanders. The brigade made air-ground coordination the pre-assignment "first lesson" precisely to ensure that new officers, from the very start of their careers (官之初), benchmark against actual combat, adjust their stride and move toward each other from the starting line, and consolidate the foundation for systems warfare.

New officers who have just stepped out of school have only recently established their professional frameworks and have not yet formed deeply ingrained patterns of thinking—this is precisely the best moment to cultivate new concepts and form new ways of thinking. Organizing them to conduct pre-assignment integration training is not only an optimization and upgrade of the training model, but also a manifestation of a profound renewal of the philosophy of personnel development.

When new platoon leaders come to understand the aerial "rules" and new pilots master the ground "language," and both sides break down the barriers between arms of service from the very beginning, truly achieving cognitive resonance on the same frequency (同频共振), this will inevitably lay a solid foundation for future coordinated combat. This also enlightens us that only by fusing the gene of coordination into the bloodstream and deeply planting the concept of integration in the heart can the "new shoots" growing side by side grow into "great trees" that support each other, consolidating the foundation of combat power for systems warfare and joint victory.

A Change of Perspective Opens Up a Vast World

■ Platoon Leader of a Brigade of the 83rd Group Army, Wang Zihao

Not long ago, as soon as the battalion's demonstration training exercise on tactical positional defense concluded, the chief of staff led us straight to the operations room to refine the action procedures, conduct sand table offense-and-defense exercises, and work through the night to draft a complete positional defense plan. Looking at this operational defense plan that closely integrated air and ground elements, my thoughts were drawn back to that pre-assignment training that left such a deep impression on me.

At the time, I had just recently graduated from the academy, my mind full of the fixed routines of ground combat; I was completely unfamiliar with this new subject of air-ground coordination, and I could not help feeling somewhat apprehensive.

In the first simulated combined exercise, my thinking shortcomings were exposed—I requested air support but failed to clearly communicate my own position, nearly causing "friendly fire" casualties. In the second combined exercise, I learned from that lesson and precisely coordinated the flight route and aerial strike plan with the pilot in a timely manner. The addition of the aerial element to the battlefield gave us wings, and we successfully completed the mission.

The different outcomes of the two combined exercises gave me a genuine appreciation of the power of air-ground coordination, and I thought I had found the "secret formula" for battlefield victory. After that, in every tactical action, I would deliberately incorporate helicopter elements. During one plan exercise, pilot Guo Xing reminded me: "Using helicopters only as transport vehicles fails to bring out the core advantage of aerial forces."

This remark made me suddenly come to my senses: treating aerial forces as a "supporting role" was, at bottom, still failing to break out of the old thinking of flat-plane warfare (平面作战). That evening, I wrote a line in my notebook: In air-ground coordination, thinking must first complete the assault from the ground to the air—one must absolutely never (决不) "board a new ship with an old ticket."

From that point on, I took every free moment to seek guidance from pilots on airspace knowledge and coordination rules, and on the training ground I worked with them to fill each other's gaps and refine tactics. Gradually I broke down the professional barriers, deepened mutual understanding, and gained a deeper appreciation of the battlefield.

A change of perspective opens up a vast world. On the future battlefield, three-dimensional warfare is the norm—ground forces cannot focus only on the ground, and pilots cannot think only about the sky. Only by breaking down walls with one heart and advancing toward war (向战而行) can we clench fingers into a fist and truly bring to bear the powerful combined strength of coordinated combat.
